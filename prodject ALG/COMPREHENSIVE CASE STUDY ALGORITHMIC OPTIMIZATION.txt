1. INTRODUCTION: OBJECTIVE AND METHODOLOGY OF THE CASE STUDY
================================================================================
Short‑form vertical video platforms (YouTube Shorts, TikTok, Instagram Reels) have
become the dominant channel of digital communication, reaching over 3 billion
active users. The effectiveness of marketing campaigns on these platforms is
determined not only by content quality but – first and foremost – by the algorithms’
ability to “notice” and “amplify” it. Yet the internal logic of these systems
remains a “black box”: decisions about who sees a video and when are made by
complex multi‑stage models that are inaccessible to external auditing.

This case study combines:
- Systemic analysis of recommendation algorithm architecture (8 levels from
  physical infrastructure to regulatory constraints).
- Mathematical forecasting models (regression, survival analysis,
  Bayesian updating).
- A detailed framework for statistical data collection and content adaptation.
- Methodology for A/B testing and iterative optimization.
- Practical case studies and a forecast of algorithm evolution through 2030.

The material is intended for an international marketing agency planning large‑scale
short‑video campaigns and can serve both as a strategic guide and an operational
checklist.

2. MULTI‑LEVEL ARCHITECTURE OF THE RECOMMENDATION ALGORITHM
================================================================================
The decision‑making process for showing a video can be decomposed into eight
sequential levels, each with its own mathematical model and set of signals.

**2.1. Level 1: Physical Infrastructure and Delivery**
At this level the fundamental feasibility of delivering content to a user is determined.
Key factors:
- **Geographic server placement** – affects latency (RTT) and content availability
  in different regions. Platforms use CDNs (Content Delivery Networks) with thousands
  of nodes, minimizing delays to <100 ms in developed regions.
- **Channel bandwidth** – limits the number of simultaneous impressions. During
  peak hours (7–11PM local time) queues may form.
- **Caching** – previously uploaded videos have an advantage because they are
  already present on edge nodes. Loading a new video can take 300–500 ms longer,
  which influences the algorithm’s decision to show it (longer load times reduce
  the probability that a user will wait for playback).

*Practical implication:* launching a campaign during peak hours (7–11 PM) may
increase competition for feed slots but also reach more users. For short videos,
latency is critical: a 1second increase in load time reduces the likelihood of
viewing by 15–20%.

**2.2. Level 2: Signal Collection and Processing**
At this level, platforms build multi‑dimensional vectors describing the user,
content, and context.

*2.2.1. User Signals (Profile)*
- **Explicit:** likes, comments, subscriptions, saves, shares.
- **Implicit:** watch time (retention), scroll‑past, repeat views, profile clicks,
  ad interactions.
- **Demographic:** age, gender, language, location (based on IP, GPS, language settings).
- **Historical:** entire interaction history (up to 3–5 years) compressed into an
  embedding (vector size 128–512), updated daily.

*2.2.2. Content Signals*
- **Metadata:** duration, resolution, hashtags, description, language, publication date,
  creator.
- **Audio‑visual features:** extracted by neural networks – mood (positive/negative),
  presence of faces, scene dynamics, presence of text, musical characteristics.
- **Behavioural:** impression and interaction history for that video (overall and
  by audience segments).

*2.2.3. Contextual Signals*
- **Time:** hour, day of week, season.
- **Device:** type (iOS/Android), model, screen size, battery level, connection type
  (Wi‑Fi/5G).
- **Location:** country, city, location type (home/work/transport).

*2.2.4. Social Graph*
- Subscriptions, mutual follows, frequency of interactions with specific creators,
  friends’ engagement with the same content.

*Mathematical model:* each entity (user, content, context) is represented as a
vector in a d‑dimensional space (d = 128–1024). Similarity between vectors is
computed as cosine similarity or dot product.

**2.3. Level 3: Candidate Generation (Retrieval)**
Task: from a pool of hundreds of millions or billions of videos, select a few
thousand that will be considered in the next stage.

*2.3.1. Methods*
- **Vector search:** uses ANN (Approximate Nearest Neighbors) with indices like
  HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) or Faiss. Search time <10 ms per
  billion objects.
- **Channels (sources):**  
  – *Subscriptions:* 10–20% of candidates from channels the user subscribes to.
  – *Trends:* videos with high viral coefficients over the last 24–72 hours.
  – *Similar users:* collaborative filtering (users with similar profiles watched
    these videos).
  – *Geolocation:* content popular in the same region.
  – *Thematic clusters:* based on hashtags, sounds, visual features.

*2.3.2. Channel Weights*
Different sources have different weights, which may be adaptive. For example,
TikTok prioritises trends (up to 50% of candidates), YouTube Shorts prioritises
subscriptions (30–40%), Instagram Reels prioritises the social graph (40–50%).

**2.4. Level 4: Predictive Ranking**
This level is the core of the algorithm. For each candidate (typically 1000–5000),
a neural network model predicts probabilities for various outcomes.

*2.4.1. Model Architecture*
Modern platforms use multi‑layer perceptrons with attention or transformers.
Input is the concatenation of user, content, context embeddings and cross‑features
(e.g., element‑wise products). Output is a vector of probabilities for k target actions.

*2.4.2. Target Metrics*
- **p_watch** – probability the user will start watching.
- **p_retention_3s** – conditional probability of watching at least 3 seconds.
- **p_retention_15s** – conditional probability of watching at least 15 seconds.
- **p_like**, **p_share**, **p_save**, **p_follow** – probabilities of social actions.
- **p_negative** – probability of a report or a scroll‑past without interaction.

*2.4.3. Multi‑Target Optimization*
The final score is computed as a weighted sum of log‑probabilities or a linear
combination of expected values:

\[
\text{score}(c) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} w_i \cdot \mathbb{E}[value_i(c)]
\]

The weights \(w_i\) are determined by A/B testing and may differ across platforms.
For instance, TikTok may give high weight to p_share, while YouTube Shorts weights
p_retention_15s more heavily.

*2.4.4. Normalization and Scaling*
To compare videos of different lengths, retention is normalised against an expected
baseline. For a 15‑second video, 50% retention might be rated higher than 40%
retention for a 60‑second video, even if absolute numbers are lower.

**2.5. Level 5: Policies and Business Constraints**
After the score is obtained, additional adjustments are applied.

*2.5.1. Diversification*
- **Temporal diversification:** the same creator cannot occupy more than 2–3
  consecutive positions in the feed.
- **Thematic diversification:** the algorithm may artificially reduce the score
  of videos from one category to avoid creating a “bubble”.
- **Social diversification:** videos from friends and popular creators may receive
  a bonus to encourage social connectivity.

*2.5.2. Monetization*
- **Ad placements:** sponsored videos may have priority in certain slots
  (e.g., every 5–7 organic impressions – one ad).
- **Paid promotion:** if a campaign is paid, the score is increased by an
  additional term proportional to the bid (CPM).

*2.5.3. Safety*
- **Moderation:** content that violates policies (violence, disinformation,
  copyright) is blocked at this level (score = 0).
- **Ethical filter:** the algorithm may lower the score for content that receives
  many complaints (even if it technically does not violate policies).

**2.6. Level 6: Experimentation and Feedback**
Platforms constantly test new model variants.

*2.6.1. A/B Testing*
Users are split into groups (e.g., 1% of traffic) where different weights \(w_i\)
or different architectures are applied. Metrics (DAU, time in app, revenue) are
compared, and the best variant is scaled.

*2.6.2. Online Learning*
Models are updated in real time (with a delay of minutes to hours) based on new
interactions. This allows rapid adaptation to changing trends.

*2.6.3. Bandit Algorithms*
For choosing among several options (e.g., different promo‑package sizes),
multi‑armed bandits may be used to optimise long‑term value.

**2.7. Level 7: Socio‑Cultural Context**
Although this level is difficult to formalise, it influences ranking effectiveness
through feedback.

- **Trends:** videos using viral sounds receive a boosted score (based on historical
  high engagement).
- **Cultural codes:** algorithms learn from billions of examples and “know” that
  certain types of content (humor, plot twists) have higher retention.
- **Herd effect:** if a video has already shown high virality among friends or
  in a region, it receives a bonus (so‑called “social proof”).

**2.8. Level 8: Regulatory and Ethical Framework**
This external framework sets boundaries for what is permissible.

- **Legislation:** GDPR (data protection), DSA (Digital Services Act), copyright,
  age restrictions.
- **Platform policies:** bans on certain categories (e.g., medical disinformation),
  requirements for algorithmic transparency.
- **Public pressure:** platforms may introduce additional filters to prevent the
  spread of harmful content, even if formally not prohibited.

3. MATHEMATICAL MODEL OF RANKING (DETAILED)
================================================================================
To understand the influence of various factors on the final score, a simplified
yet representative mathematical model is useful.

Let:
- \(u \in \mathbb{R}^d\) – user embedding.
- \(c \in \mathbb{R}^d\) – content embedding.
- \(x \in \mathbb{R}^p\) – vector of contextual features.
- \(s \in \mathbb{R}^q\) – vector of social features (e.g., number of friends who
  have seen the video).

The prediction model for target metrics can be expressed as:

\[
p_j = \sigma\left( \text{MLP}_j\left( [u; c; x; s; u \odot c; \dots] \right) \right)
\]

where \(\sigma\) is the sigmoid, MLP is a multi‑layer perceptron, \(\odot\) is
element‑wise product.

Final score:

\[
\text{score}(c) = \sum_{j=1}^{k} w_j \cdot \log\left( \frac{p_j}{1-p_j} \right) + \lambda \cdot \text{boost}(c) - \mu \cdot \text{penalty}(c)
\]

Here \(\log(p/(1-p))\) is the logit, often used for linear combination of probabilities
because it can range from \(-\infty\) to \(+\infty\). \(\text{boost}(c)\) is a bonus
for paid promotion, \(\text{penalty}(c)\) is a penalty for diversity or safety.

The weights \(w_j\), \(\lambda\), \(\mu\) are hyperparameters optimised based on
business metrics (DAU, revenue, time in app).

**3.1. Sensitivity Analysis**
- **Criticality of weights:** even a 5% change in a weight can alter the top‑10
  composition by 30–50%.
- **Threshold effects:** for display, the absolute score matters less than its
  relative position among candidates. Hence competition for impressions is relative.
- **Temporal dynamics:** a video’s score decays rapidly after the first hours
  (decay constant τ ≈ 24–48 hours for TikTok, 7 days for YouTube Shorts).

4. PLATFORM IMPLEMENTATIONS: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS
================================================================================
Based on patent documents and technical publications, the following differences
stand out.

**4.1. TikTok**
- **Early amplification:** the first 200–500 impressions are crucial. The algorithm
  uses a method close to a “bandit”, quickly cutting low‑performing videos.
- **Virality as goal:** the primary metric is the K‑factor (number of additional
  views generated by each view). This leads the algorithm to actively seek content
  that can spread beyond subscribers.
- **Model features:** architecture with strong attention to audio features
  (neural network trained on millions of audio tracks).
- **Lifetime:** 24–48 hours. After that, the score drops by an order of magnitude.

**4.2. YouTube Shorts**
- **Retention priority:** models aim for long retention (retention at 15, 30 seconds).
  The weight for p_share is lower than on TikTok.
- **Subscription‑based retrieval:** up to 40% of candidates come from channels the
  user is subscribed to.
- **Contextual relevance:** search and long‑video watch history on YouTube are
  considered, giving deeper insight into interests.
- **Lifetime:** 7–10 days. Older videos may still get impressions if they show high
  retention within their niche.

**4.3. Instagram Reels**
- **Social connectivity:** strong bonus if a video has already been saved or shared
  by friends. The weight for p_save may be 2–3 times higher than others.
- **Integration with feed:** Reels impressions are mixed with friends’ posts and ads,
  creating additional competition.
- **Lower sensitivity to length:** the algorithm is less strict about early‑second
  retention than TikTok, but responds more strongly to saves and shares.

5. STATISTICAL METHODS AND DATA COLLECTION FRAMEWORK
================================================================================
To objectively assess algorithmic effectiveness, systematic data collection
throughout the content lifecycle is necessary.

**5.1. Data Sources**
- **Official platform APIs:**
  – YouTube Analytics API (views, retention, traffic sources)
  – TikTok Business API (virality, demographics, audience)
  – Instagram Insights API (reach, interactions, saves)
- **Third‑party analytics tools:**
  – Social Blade (historical channel data)
  – HypeAuditor (audience verification, engagement indices)
- **Own campaign data:**
  – UTM tags, A/B tests, internal databases with video metadata.

**5.2. Storage Structure (Normalised Schema)**
| Entity          | Attributes                                                                 |
|-----------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Video**       | video_id, platform, duration, publish_timestamp, tags, audio_id,           |
|                 | thumbnail_hash, category, language, creator_id                             |
| **View statistics** | date, hour, impressions, views, retention_3s, retention_15s, retention_30s, |
|                 | likes, comments, shares, saves, follows, avg_view_duration, K_factor       |
| **Channel**     | channel_id, subscribers, total_views, engagement_rate, posting_frequency,  |
|                 | niche, avg_retention, top_performing_formats                               |

**5.3. Collection Frequency and Aggregation**
- **Micro‑level:** data collected every hour for the first 48 hours after publication,
  then once daily.
- **Macro‑level:** aggregated by day, week, month to detect trends.
- **Anomalies:** outliers (e.g., sudden spike in views) are flagged and analysed
  separately (may be due to algorithmic amplification or external factors).

**5.4. Key Statistical Metrics**
- **Retention curves:** R(t) – fraction of users who watch up to time t.
  Approximated by exponential‑power functions to assess content “stickiness”.
- **Viral coefficient K:** K = (views from non‑subscribers) / (views from subscribers).
  K > 2.0 indicates strong viral potential.
- **Time decay:** S(t) = S₀·e^{-t/τ} + S_base. The time constant τ is a key
  platform characteristic.
- **Traffic source analysis:** distribution of impressions by channel (subscriptions,
  recommendations, external, search).

6. CONTENT ADAPTATION TO ALGORITHMIC SIGNALS
================================================================================
Based on the identified patterns, practical recommendations for creating content
that maximises the likelihood of being shown are formulated.

**6.1. Design of the First 3 Seconds (Critical Ranking Factor)**
- **Visual hook:** bright colour, motion, face with emotion, unexpected object.
- **Audio hook:** loud sound, musical accent, unconventional voice.
- **Text hook:** question, intrigue, promise of benefit.
*Statistics:* videos with a clear hook within the first 0.5seconds have 35–50%
higher 3‑second retention.

**6.2. Retention Structure**
Ideal profile:
- 0–3s: hook, retention >80%
- 3–15s: story development, scene changes, maintain interest (>50%)
- 15–30s: climax, resolution, call‑to‑action (>30%)
- 30–60s: only if the content is exceptionally useful or entertaining.

**6.3. Use of Audio**
- **Trending sounds:** using them boosts virality by 2–3× (2025–2026 data).
  The algorithm “remembers” the sound and associates it with successful videos.
- **Original sounds:** can become viral but take longer to gain traction.
- **Cross‑platform adaptation:** a sound viral on TikTok transfers to Reels with
  a coefficient of 0.6–0.7.

**6.4. Text and Subtitles**
- **Title/description:** keywords for search and semantic analysis. On YouTube
  Shorts, the title influences retrieval.
- **Subtitles:** increase retention by 10–15% (especially for educational content).
- **Hashtags:** 3–5 relevant + 1–2 broad. Over‑tagging (>10) reduces effectiveness.

**6.5. Visual Aesthetics and Branding**
- **Consistent style:** logo, colour scheme, fonts – the algorithm more easily
  identifies the channel.
- **Face in frame:** videos with faces have 30% higher retention (the “social
  presence” effect).

7. A/B TESTING METHODOLOGY AND ITERATIVE OPTIMIZATION
================================================================================
A formal protocol is proposed to isolate the influence of each factor and
continuously improve campaign effectiveness based on data.

**7.1. A/B Test Protocol**
1. **Hypothesis:** e.g., “changing the music track will increase retention”.
2. **Variants:** control (original) and test (modified track).
3. **Audience split:** random 50/50 assignment, ensuring comparable demographic groups.
4. **Measurement:** retention at 3, 15, 30 seconds, viral coefficient K.
5. **Statistical significance:** two‑sample t‑test, α = 0.05.

**7.2. Multi‑Armed Bandits for Multiple Variants**
When comparing 5–10 variants, bandit algorithms (Thompson sampling) are more
efficient, automatically allocating more traffic to better options while still
collecting data.

**7.3. Iterative Improvement**
After identifying the best variant, the next cycle begins:
- Improvement of 10–20% on key metrics.
- Re‑testing with new hypotheses.

**7.4. Example Optimization Cycle**
1. Original video: 3s retention = 55%, K = 1.2.
2. Test A: add text overlay on seconds 1–3 → 3s retention = 67%, K = 1.5.
3. Test B: change background music to trending → 3s retention = 63%, K = 2.1.
4. Combination: text + trending music → 3s retention = 71%, K = 2.6.
5. Final variant is scaled.

8. CASE STUDIES (SYNTHETIC DATA BASED ON REAL PATTERNS)
================================================================================
**Case 1: Successful TikTok Campaign**
- **Goal:** promote a language‑learning mobile app.
- **Strategy:** 5 video variants (15s) with different approaches:
  – Control: standard promo.
  – Variant A: used trending sound + text “3 words that will change your life”.
  – Variant B: challenge “repeat after me” with call to comment.
- **Results:** Variant A achieved retention_3s = 78%, K = 3.2.
  Variant B – retention_3s = 65%, K = 2.1.
- **Conclusion:** strong hook (text + trending sound) outweighed interactivity.
- **Scaling:** $5,000 budget on Variant A delivered 2.1M views, app install cost
  $0.35 (40% below market average).

**Case 2: YouTube Shorts – Educational Channel**
- **Goal:** increase subscribers on a physics channel.
- **Strategy:** series of 10 videos (30–45s) with different structures:
  – Strict scientific style (control).
  – With added humour and unexpected analogies.
- **Results:** humorous videos had retention_15s = 55%, control – 35%.
  Subscription conversion – 2.1% vs 0.8%.
- **Conclusion:** even for educational content, emotional appeal is critical.

**Case 3: Instagram Reels – Fashion Brand**
- **Goal:** increase brand awareness.
- **Strategy:** three formats:
  – Behind‑the‑scenes shoots.
  – User‑generated content (UGC) with hashtag.
  – Product reviews with music.
- **Results:** UGC showed the highest saves (12% of views), leading to prolonged
  presence in the recommendation feed.
- **Conclusion:** content that users save receives the greatest algorithmic bonus
  on Reels.

9. FORECAST OF ALGORITHM EVOLUTION (2026–2030)
================================================================================
Based on patent filings and public developer statements, the following trends emerge.

**9.1. Increased Transparency**
Under regulatory pressure (DSA, AI Act), platforms will be forced to disclose more
information about ranking factors. This will create opportunities for more precise
optimisation.

**9.2. Shift from Quantitative to Qualitative Metrics**
Instead of likes and views, “usefulness” of content will gain importance – e.g.,
the ability to spark meaningful discussions, learning, social cooperation.

**9.3. Integration of Symbiotic Interfaces**
Users will be able to customise their “cognitive profiles” (e.g., prioritising
educational content), making algorithms more predictable and manageable.

**9.4. Enhanced Role of Context**
Algorithms will consider not only behaviour but also the real‑world environment
(location, mood, even biometric data) to select content suited to the user’s
current state.

================================================================================
10. CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
================================================================================
This case study shows that recommendation algorithms on short‑video platforms
are hierarchical multi‑level systems, where each level contributes to the final
decision. The key factors determining campaign effectiveness are:
- **Ability to pass initial retrieval** (entering the candidate pool).
- **High retention in the first 3–15 seconds** (critical signal for predictive models).
- **Creation of social connectivity** (involving friends, saves, shares).
- **Alignment with cultural context and trends** (using viral sounds, memes,
  expectation‑inversion).

**For the international marketing agency, it is recommended to:**
1. **Establish an algorithmic analysis lab**, including data scientists, analysts,
   and creators, for systematic data collection and processing.
2. **Implement an A/B testing protocol** using bandit algorithms for rapid
   hypothesis validation.
3. **Develop an internal database of successful patterns** (hooks, retention
   structures, audio tracks) linked to each platform.
4. **Invest in automated data collection** (APIs, dashboards) for hourly monitoring
   in the first 48 hours after publication.
5. **Account for long‑term trends** – shift toward qualitative metrics and symbiotic
   interfaces, beginning now to create content that delivers value and educational
   benefit.

The proposed framework reduces uncertainty, cuts testing budgets, and improves the
predictability of campaign outcomes in a fiercely competitive attention economy.

APPENDIX A: GLOSSARY OF TERMS
================================================================================
- **Impression** – the fact that a video is delivered to a user’s feed
  (no interaction required).
- **Retrieval** – the stage of selecting thousands of candidates from the overall pool.
- **Ranking** – the stage of sorting candidates by predicted value.
- **Embedding** – a dense vector representation of an entity (user, content) in
  a high‑dimensional space.
- **ANN (Approximate Nearest Neighbors)** – algorithms for fast approximate nearest
  neighbour search used in retrieval.
- **Multi‑target optimization** – an approach where a model is trained to predict
  several target metrics simultaneously, which are then combined with weights.
- **Cognitive inversion** – a technique based on breaking audience expectations
  within a recognisable cultural context.
