**5.2. ECONOMIC THEORY**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Economic theory serves as the foundation for understanding consumer behaviour,
market mechanisms, and macroeconomic factors affecting the demand for metal products.
The models in this section are used by agents for calibrating forecasts, pricing,
and strategic planning.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**5.2.1. MICROECONOMICS OF DEMAND**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**5.2.1.1. Consumer utility function**
A buyer of metal products (individual or construction team) chooses a product
maximising utility under a budget constraint. Utility is described by the function:

\[
U = \alpha \cdot \text{quality} + \beta \cdot \text{price} + \gamma \cdot \text{delivery} + \varepsilon
\]

where \(\varepsilon\) is a random component. In aggregated form this leads to the
logit demand model (see 5.1.2).

**5.2.1.2. Price and income elasticity of demand**
- **Price elasticity:** for metal products (pickets, angle brackets) demand is
  inelastic at peak season (|E| < 1) and moderately elastic in off‑season (|E| ~ 1.2).
  For new products elasticity is higher.
- **Income elasticity:** building materials have positive but moderate income
  elasticity (0.5–0.8), as they are considered necessity goods for developers.

**5.2.1.3. Substitution effect**
When picket prices rise, buyers may switch to alternative materials (corrugated sheet,
wood). Substitution elasticity is estimated from competitor data and is used in the
logit model through the “competitor price” variable.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**5.2.2. MACROECONOMIC FACTORS**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The macroeconomic environment affects demand through several channels:

| Factor                     | Impact on metal product sales                                            |
|----------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Construction activity**  | Growth in residential and commercial construction directly correlates with sales (coefficient 0.6–0.8). Source: Rosstat, housing commissioning data. |
| **Inflation**              | Rising inflation increases metal costs, forcing price increases; demand may decline under high inflation (income effect). |
| **Central Bank key rate**  | Affects credit availability for construction companies and individual developers. Rate increases reduce investment in construction, which affects demand with a lag (6–9 months). |
| **Seasonality (climate)**  | Construction season in Russia: April–October. Seasonal coefficients: March 1.1, April 1.3, May 1.4, June–August 1.2, September 1.1, October 1.0, November–February 0.7–0.9. |
| **Holiday periods**        | May holidays – sales decline (0.8), December – pre‑New Year surge (1.2). |

**Macro factor forecast**  
For quarterly forecasts, extrapolation of macro indicators is used based on data
from the Ministry of Economic Development and consensus forecasts. Impact coefficients
are estimated by regression and updated quarterly.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**5.2.3. BEHAVIOURAL ECONOMICS**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**5.2.3.1. Impact of reviews and ratings**
Positive reviews increase purchase probability (social proof effect). Empirically:
a 0.5‑point increase in rating (on a 5‑point scale) increases conversion by 8–12%.
Negative reviews (rating ≤3) reduce sales by 15–20% for 2–3 weeks unless remedial
action is taken (reply, correction).

**5.2.3.2. Loss aversion**
Buyers are more sensitive to price increases than to price decreases. This is taken
into account in dynamic pricing: a price increase of more than 10% over a short
period (less than 7 days) reduces demand by 25% (effect exceeding elasticity).

**5.2.3.3. Decision‑making under uncertainty**
Buyers on Wildberries make decisions with limited information. The “Pricing strategist”
agent uses this by suggesting prices ending in “99”, which increases conversion by
5–7% (left‑digit effect).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**5.2.4. INDUSTRIAL ORGANISATION (THEORY OF INDUSTRY MARKETS)**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**5.2.4.1. Structure of the metal products market on Wildberries**
The market is characterised by:
- **Monopolistic competition** – many sellers, product differentiation (shape,
  colour, coating, price).
- **Low entry barriers** – registration on WB, raw material purchase, setting up
  production.
- **High price sensitivity** in standard categories (angle brackets, strips) and
  differentiation in decorative pickets.

**5.2.4.2. Demand curve for a typical product**
For each SKU, the “Competitive intelligence” agent constructs a demand curve
(sales as a function of price) based on historical data and regression. The curve
is used to determine the optimal price (see 5.1.2).

**5.2.4.3. Market power and pricing**
For unique products (exclusive design) market power is higher, allowing a higher
markup (up to 70%). For standard products, the markup is limited by competitor
prices (usually 25–35%).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**5.2.5. GAME THEORY IN COMPETITIVE INTERACTION**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**5.2.5.1. Price war game**
When one competitor lowers its price, others are forced to react. The model predicts
that if the reduction does not exceed 5%, others may keep prices; for larger
reductions a chain reaction occurs. The “Pricing strategist” agent uses this rule
to choose the reaction threshold (default 5%).

**5.2.5.2. Cooperative equilibrium (tacit collusion)**
On Wildberries, price stability is often observed in categories with a few large
sellers. The agent analyses the frequency of price changes and, if no sharp movements
occur for 2 weeks, may increase the markup by 2–3% (testing).

**5.2.5.3. Leader‑follower strategy**
The largest seller in a category often acts as the price leader. The agent identifies
the leader (by sales volume) and uses its price as a benchmark, setting its own price
2–5% lower or higher depending on differentiation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**5.2.6. ECONOMIC MECHANISMS WITHIN THE WILDBERRIES PLATFORM**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

| Mechanism                | Impact on sales and strategy                                               |
|--------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **WB commissions**       | 10–15% of product price. Affect net margin; the agent accounts for them when calculating the optimal price. |
| **Promotions and discounts** | Participating in promotions increases visibility but reduces margin. The agent evaluates promotion ROI and recommends participation only when expected revenue increase >20%. |
| **Rating and reviews**   | Affect search ranking. The “Customer service” agent maintains a high rating through automated replies and negative feedback handling. |
| **Paid advertising**     | Internal advertising (auction) – the “Ad optimiser” agent manages bids to maximise ROMI. |

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**5.2.7. PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION IN AGENTS**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

- **“Demand analyst” agent** uses macroeconomic factors and seasonal coefficients
  to adjust forecasts (via Prophet with regressors).
- **“Pricing strategist” agent** applies the logit model, accounting for elasticity
  and substitution effects, as well as game‑theory rules.
- **“Risk manager” agent** tracks changes in macro indicators (rate, inflation)
  and triggers a strategy review for significant deviations.
