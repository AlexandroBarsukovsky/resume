**5.1. DEMAND FORECASTING MODEL (FORECAST ENGINE)**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**5.1.1. Purpose and Input Data**
Demand forecasting is the foundation for production planning, procurement, and advertising budgets. The model builds forecasts for 7, 30, and 90 days for each SKU.

| Input Parameter           | Source                                   | Update Frequency |
|---------------------------|------------------------------------------|------------------|
| Daily sales (units)       | WB API Monitor (table `orders`)          | Daily            |
| Price (RUB)               | WB API / “Pricing strategist” agent      | On change        |
| Seasonal coefficients     | “Seasonal forecaster” agent              | Monthly          |
| Weather data              | OpenWeatherMap (temperature, precipitation) | Daily         |
| Holiday calendar          | Built‑in calendar (January, May holidays)| Static           |
| Promotions / discounts    | Advertising campaign data                | As incurred      |

**5.1.2. Ensemble Architecture**
A combination of three models is used with dynamic weight adjustment:

1. **Prophet (Facebook)** – baseline, handles trends, seasonality, holidays well.
2. **ARIMA (auto.arima)** – for short‑term forecasts (7 days), sensitive to local fluctuations.
3. **Exponential smoothing (Holt‑Winters)** – for medium‑term forecasts (30–90 days), multiplicative seasonality.

Model weights are optimised monthly to minimise MAPE on a test set (last 30 days). The final forecast formula:

\[
\hat{y}_t = w_1 \cdot \hat{y}_t^{(Prophet)} + w_2 \cdot \hat{y}_t^{(ARIMA)} + w_3 \cdot \hat{y}_t^{(HW)}
\]

where \(w_i \ge 0\), \(\sum w_i = 1\). Optimal weights are found by brute force with a step of 0.1.

**5.1.3. Accounting for Seasonality and External Factors**
- **Seasonality:** multiplicative STL decomposition (statsmodels) extracts weekly, monthly, and yearly seasonality.
- **Weather adjustments:** linear regression on historical data:

\[
\text{Sales}_d = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot T_d + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Precipitation}_d + \varepsilon
\]

Coefficients are recalculated monthly. For future days, forecast weather values are used.

- **Holiday effects:** fixed coefficients (e.g., during May holidays, picket sales drop by 20%). Coefficients are extracted from historical data.

**5.1.4. Accuracy Assessment**
\[
\text{MAPE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^{n} \left| \frac{y_t - \hat{y}_t}{y_t} \right| \times 100\%
\]

Target: MAPE < 15% for 30‑day forecasts. If the threshold of 20% is exceeded, model retraining and input data quality checks are triggered.

**5.2. OPTIMAL PRICING MODEL (PRICING AGENT)**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**5.2.1. Logit Model for Purchase Probability**
The probability that a customer will buy a product at price \(p\) given competitor price \(p_c\) and rating \(r\) is described by logistic regression:

\[
P(\text{purchase}) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\alpha_0 + \alpha_1 p + \alpha_2 p_c + \alpha_3 r)}}
\]

Coefficients \(\alpha\) are estimated from historical data (sales and prices over the last 6 months) using maximum likelihood. The model is recalculated monthly.

**5.2.2. Margin Optimisation**
The objective is to maximise expected marginal profit subject to a minimum margin constraint \(m_{\min}\):

\[
\max_{p} \quad (p - c) \cdot Q(p) \cdot P_{\text{purchase}}(p)
\]

where:
- \(c\) – cost,
- \(Q(p)\) – forecasted demand at price \(p\) (from the demand model, Section 5.1),
- \(P_{\text{purchase}}(p)\) – purchase probability from the logit model.

The solution is found numerically (golden section search) on the interval \([c \cdot (1+m_{\min}), \, \max(\text{competitor\_price}, c \cdot (1+m_{\max})]\).

**5.2.3. Manual Intervention Rules**
- If deviation from the current price >20% – the decision is not applied automatically but sent to the console for confirmation.
- During a promotion (discount >10%), the model is temporarily disabled, and the price is fixed for the promotion period.
- The price cannot be lower than cost + 10% (minimum margin).

**5.3. INVENTORY MANAGEMENT MODEL (INVENTORY OPTIMIZER)**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**5.3.1. (s, S) Model with Uncertainty**
For each SKU, two thresholds are calculated:
- **s** – the level at which an order should be placed,
- **S** – the target replenishment level.

Parameters are computed based on demand forecast and lead time (L = 14 days for raw materials, 7 days for finished goods at WB).

**5.3.2. Formulas**
Mean demand during lead time: \(\mu_L = \hat{y}_{L} \cdot L\) (where \(\hat{y}_{L}\) is the forecast for the lead time period). Standard deviation of demand during lead time: \(\sigma_L = \sqrt{L \cdot \sigma^2}\) (\(\sigma\) – standard deviation of daily demand).

Service level (probability of no stockout) is set to 95%, corresponding to safety factor \(z = 1.645\).

\[
s = \mu_L + z \cdot \sigma_L
\]

\[
S = \mu_L + z \cdot \sigma_L + Q_{\text{opt}}
\]

where \(Q_{\text{opt}}\) is the economic order quantity (Wilson formula):

\[
Q_{\text{opt}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \cdot \mu_L \cdot K}{h}}
\]

- \(K\) – fixed order cost (≈ 5000 RUB),
- \(h\) – holding cost per unit per day (≈ 0.5% of cost).

**5.3.3. Operation Algorithm**
1. Daily check of stock level (production + WB).
2. If stock < s – a production order (or raw material purchase) is generated in the amount of \(S - \text{stock}\).
3. The order is sent to agent D3 “Inventory manager” for execution.
4. If a shortage occurs (stock = 0), an urgent alert is sent to the operator.

**5.4. BUSINESS HEALTH SCORE (HEALTH SCORE)**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**5.4.1. Composite Indicators**
\[
H = w_1 \cdot (1 - \text{shortage}) + w_2 \cdot \text{turnover}_\text{norm} + w_3 \cdot \text{margin}_\text{norm}
\]

Normalisation:
- **shortage** – proportion of days in the month when the product was out of stock (0–1).
- **turnover_norm** – \(1 - \frac{\text{turnover} - \text{target}}{\text{target}}\), clipped to [0,1].
- **margin_norm** – ratio of current margin to target (35%).

Weights were obtained via analytic hierarchy process (AHP) with the owner’s participation: \(w_1 = 0.4\), \(w_2 = 0.3\), \(w_3 = 0.3\).

**5.4.2. Colour Zones and Playbook**
| H              | Zone     | Action                                     |
|----------------|----------|--------------------------------------------|
| ≥ 0.7          | Green    | Monitoring, planned expansion              |
| 0.5 ≤ H < 0.7  | Yellow   | Daily review, price/procurement adjustments|
| < 0.5          | Red      | Urgent playbook: root cause analysis, manual intervention, escalation |

Health Score is recalculated daily and displayed on the main console dashboard.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**5.5. TASK PRIORITISATION MODEL (TASK SCORE)**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

To rank tasks requiring operator attention (confirmation queue), the following formula is used:

\[
T = w_{\text{urgency}} \cdot \text{urgency} + w_{\text{impact}} \cdot \text{impact} + w_{\text{resources}} \cdot (1 - \text{availability})
\]

Parameters:
- **urgency** – inverse of time until deadline (0–1),
- **impact** – estimated impact on revenue (0–1) (high – 1, medium – 0.5, low – 0.1),
- **availability** – resource readiness (0–1) (e.g., raw material stock, budget).

Weights are set by the operator (default \(w_{\text{urgency}}=0.5, w_{\text{impact}}=0.3, w_{\text{resources}}=0.2\)). Tasks with T > 0.7 are displayed in red in the queue.

**5.6. NEW IDEA GENERATION MODEL (GENESIS)**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**5.6.1. Idea Sources**
- Review analysis (identified wishes, defects).
- Competitor data (new products, popular items).
- Trends (from social media, search queries).
- Random combinations of existing SKUs (hybrid products).

**5.6.2. Filtering Process**
1. Generate 20–30 ideas per week (LLM + rules).
2. Automatic filtering by manufacturability (agent E2 “Process engineer”).
3. Calculation of expected margin (similar to pricing model).
4. A/B testing: 3–5 ideas are launched in a pilot batch (10–20 units) on WB.
5. Based on test results (conversion, reviews), a decision is made to scale.

**5.6.3. Success Metric**
\[
\text{Idea success} = \frac{\text{revenue from idea over 30 days} - \text{cost}}{\text{cost}} \times 100\%
\]

If success > 50%, the idea is added to the permanent assortment.
