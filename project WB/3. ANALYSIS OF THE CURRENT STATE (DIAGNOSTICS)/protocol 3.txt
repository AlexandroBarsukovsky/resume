**3.1. ORGANISATIONAL STRUCTURE**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**3.1.1. Overall management structure**
The organisation is a classic small manufacturing enterprise with centralised management. Key roles:

| Role                 | Number | Functions                                                                 | Load (hours/week) |
|----------------------|--------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------|
| **Owner**            | 1      | Strategic planning, production management, sales control,                 | 50–60             |
|                      |        | marketplace management, finance, taxes, raw material procurement, advertising |                   |
| **Production team**  | 8      | Manufacturing (bending, welding, painting), packaging, shipment           | 40 (each)         |
| **Accountant (outsourced)** | 1  | Quarterly tax calculation, return preparation                            | 2 (per month)     |
| **Logistics (outsourced)** | 1   | Raw material delivery, shipping finished goods to Wildberries warehouse  | 5 (per month)     |

**3.1.2. Distribution of authority characteristics**
- The owner simultaneously performs **operational, managerial, and strategic** functions, leading to overload and reduced decision quality.
- The production team works a fixed schedule (5/2) and has no access to demand data, making production planning “blind”.
- No dedicated marketplace and advertising manager – all Wildberries tasks fall on the owner.
- The accountant receives data once a quarter, so operational margin control is absent.

**3.1.3. Communication flows**
- Owner → production: verbal instructions, occasionally notes in Excel.
- Wildberries → owner: manual export of reports from the personal account.
- Accountant → owner: quarterly transfer of Excel files with calculations.

**Key problem:** lack of a unified information environment; all decisions are made based on incomplete and outdated data.

**3.2.2. Integration analysis**
- **No automatic synchronisation** between WB, Excel, and 1С. All data is transferred manually, resulting in:
  – accounting delays (up to 2–3 days),
  – transfer errors (up to 5% discrepancies in inventory),
  – inability to perform operational analysis.
- **Excel is used as a “database”**, but lacks integrity controls, versioning, or access rights management.
- **No analytics tools** – no dashboards, forecasting models, or automatic alerts.

**3.2.3. Technical constraints**
- No API integration with Wildberries (the owner does not use the official API, working through the web interface).
- No dedicated server or cloud infrastructure.
- All data is stored locally on the owner’s PC – high risk of loss in case of hardware failure.

**3.3.1. Inventory management**

**3.3.2. Pricing**
- **Source data:** cost (Excel), WB commissions (fixed), competitor prices (once a week review of 10–20 product cards).
- **Formula:** `Price = (Cost + Delivery) × (1 + Markup)`, where markup is fixed (35–50%).
- **Adjustment:** once a week, when competitor prices are lower – manual reduction by 5–10%. If competitors raise prices, price stays the same.
- **Problems:** delayed reaction (up to 7 days), lack of elasticity consideration, suboptimal margin.

**3.3.3. Handling reviews**
- **Detection:** owner checks the “Reviews” section on WB daily, filters new ones.
- **Analysis:** reads text, assesses sentiment visually.
- **Response:** for negative ones – apology, solution offer (refund, discount). For positive – rarely.
- **Response time:** average 48 hours (sometimes up to 5 days).
- **Systematisation:** none; causes of negativity are not recorded.

**3.3.4. Advertising and promotion**
- **Participation in promotions:** owner monitors WB promotion calendar (once a month), selects items for participation, sets discounts.
- **Advertising campaigns:** every 1–2 weeks launches campaigns with fixed budget (10–30 thousand ₽) without prior analysis.
- **Analytics:** reviews WB reports on spending and sales, but does not calculate ROMI.

**3.3.5. Finance and taxes**
- **Daily accounting:** revenue recorded in Excel (summation of orders from WB).
- **Cost:** entered into Excel monthly based on purchases.
- **Taxes:** quarterly, data is sent to accountant (Excel), who prepares the return. Owner pays tax upon notification.
- **Problems:** lack of plan‑vs‑actual analysis, data transfer errors, risk of late tax payments.

4. **Forecasting metrics** (demand, raw material needs) absent.

**3.5. IDENTIFIED PAIN POINTS (DETAILED CLASSIFICATION)**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**3.5.1. Data fragmentation and information gaps**
- **Symptom:** Excel, WB, 1С, bank are not synchronised.
- **Consequences:** owner spends 10–15 hours per week on manual data transfer; accounting errors (up to 5% discrepancies); no single source of truth for decisions.

**3.5.2. Manual prioritisation and slow reaction speed**
- **Symptom:** shortage detection – visual, competitor prices – weekly, reviews – as they arrive.
- **Consequences:** seasonal shortages (15% revenue loss), off‑season overstocking, missed dynamic pricing opportunities, customer loss due to unanswered reviews.

**3.5.3. Lack of forecasting models**
- **Symptom:** raw material procurement “by eye”, production without seasonality consideration.
- **Consequences:** suboptimal use of working capital, production downtime, lost revenue.

**3.5.4. Lack of business health score**
- **Symptom:** no automatic monitoring of key indicators (shortage, margin, turnover).
- **Consequences:** problems identified post‑factum (e.g., shortage only when orders are already lost), lack of proactive management.

**3.5.5. High workload on the owner**
- **Symptom:** 35+ hours per week on routine.
- **Consequences:** lack of time for strategy (assortment expansion, new channels), burnout, risk of errors.

**3.5.6. Missed expansion opportunities**
- **Symptom:** new products 1–2 per year, no trend analysis.
- **Consequences:** loss of market share, untapped potential (up to 30% additional revenue).

**3.6. CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS AND THEIR IMPACT ON AUTOMATION**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**3.6.1. Attitude toward automation**
- Owner recognises the need for change but fears loss of control and implementation complexity.
- Willing to invest but demands transparency and the ability to manually intervene.
- Prefers simple interfaces (analogous to strategy games) – does not want to learn complex CRM systems.

**3.6.2. Decision‑making style**
- Based on intuition and personal experience rather than data.
- Requires clear visualisations (dashboards, charts) to make decisions.

**3.6.3. System requirements**
- **Transparency:** all agent decisions must be explainable and viewable.
- **Control:** owner must have the ability to override any automated action (“Undo” or “Intervene” button).
- **Phased approach:** implementation in phases, with the option to stop after each stage.
- **Training:** simple documentation and training via practical examples (plain language) needed.

**3.6.4. Culture‑related risks**
- Resistance to change if automation is perceived as a “black box”.
- Reluctance to spend time learning new tools.
- Possible distrust of agent forecasts in the early stages.

**3.6.5. Implementation strategy considering cultural characteristics**
- **Pilot on one product:** start by automating one item (pickets), show results.
- **Operator console in game style:** interface resembling a strategy game with intuitive controls.
- **Scenario‑based training:** demonstrate how agents help avoid shortages or increase margins.
- **Manual override:** an “Intervene” button on every automated decision.
