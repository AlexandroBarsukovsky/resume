**1.1. BRIEF DESCRIPTION OF THE BUSINESS AND THE PROBLEM**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Company** – a manufacturer of metal products (metal picket fences, angle brackets, wind strips, flashings). Operations are conducted on the Russian market through the Wildberries marketplace. Over 1 year and 3 months, 13,918 units were sold, 2,876 ratings were received, with an average rating of **4.9 out of 5**. The average product price is 5,548 ₽, and monthly revenue is approximately 6.1 million ₽.

**Business model** – made‑to‑order production with warehouse stock maintained for fast‑moving items. All key processes are performed manually:
- Inventory management (Excel, visual inspection).
- Pricing (cost plus fixed markup, ignoring competitors).
- Handling reviews (reading manually, occasional replies).
- Advertising campaigns (participating in Wildberries promotions, configuring without analytics).
- Financial accounting (Excel, manual tax calculation).
- Production planning (by intuition).

**Problem** – the business has reached the “ceiling of manual management”: further growth is constrained by limited human resources, revenue loss due to stockouts, missed opportunities for assortment expansion, and an inability to respond quickly to market changes. Key pain points:
1. **Data fragmentation** – information about sales, inventory, competitor prices, reviews, and advertising budgets is scattered across different sources (Excel, Wildberries personal account, bank), with no synchronisation.
2. **Manual prioritisation** – the owner spends 10–15 hours per week gathering and processing data instead of focusing on strategic development.
3. **Inaccurate forecasts** – raw material procurement and production planning are done “blindly”, leading to shortages during peak season (up to 15% lost revenue) and overstocking during off‑season (inventory turnover 45 days vs target 30).
4. **Lack of automatic control** – negative reviews go unanswered for up to 2 days, lowering ratings and conversion; competitor price attacks go unnoticed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**1.2. PROPOSED SOLUTION: AN AI ECOSYSTEM OF 38 SPECIALISED AGENTS**
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We propose building a **scalable agent platform** that automates all key business processes, leaving only strategic decisions and exception handling to humans. The architecture is based on multi‑level orchestration.

**Ecosystem composition (38 agents):**
- **6 analytical agents** (competitive intelligence, Wildberries API monitor, demand analyst, review processor, news monitor, seasonal forecaster) – data collection and initial processing.
- **6 strategic agents** (pricing strategist, assortment strategist, risk manager, marketing analyst, quality control, expansion analyst) – decision‑making based on analytics.
- **8 tactical coordinators** (4 product‑group coordinators + scriptor #1, archivist, supervisor, production scheduler) – adapting strategy to specific product groups and coordinating execution.
- **18 executive agents** (tax calculator, ad optimiser, inventory manager, logistics dispatcher, financial controller, legal assistant, customer service, document flow manager, scriptors #2–3, and 8 creative agents: designer, process engineer, creative marketer, test analyst, trend scout, system architect, partner integrator, quantum synthesizer).

**Technical implementation:**
- Event‑driven architecture – real‑time reaction to events (new review, competitor price drop, inventory change).
- Scriptor chain (#1 – core‑coalition connection, #2 – budget management, #3 – execution via Wildberries API, 1C, bank).
- Operator console – interface in the style of a strategy game (dashboards, manual override, threshold configuration).
