**3.6. CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS (HIGH AGENCY, REMOTE-FIRST) AND THEIR IMPACT ON AUTOMATION**

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.6.1. KEY PRINCIPLES OF SOURCEGRAPH’S CULTURE (FROM THE HANDBOOK)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. **High agency** – each employee acts as an owner, does not wait for instructions,
   takes responsibility for solving problems. This means people are used to
   finding their own ways, but may also feel discomfort with rigidly
   regulated processes.

2. **Direct communication** – honesty and directness, even when difficult.
   No politics, escalation through arguments. In automation,
   this requires transparency of rules and the ability to challenge
   the system’s decisions.

3. **Remote-first** – the company has been fully remote since 2019. All processes
   are built on written communication, documentation, asynchronous discussions.
   Automation must support written interfaces and enable working without
   synchronous meetings.

4. **Transparency** – salaries, levels, OKRs, plans, even financial information
   are open to all employees. This means that data used in automation
   (e.g., forecasts) must be accessible and understandable to all
   stakeholders.

5. **Love for developers** – deep understanding of developers’ pain points, respect
   for their craft. GTM automation should not create a feeling of “bureaucracy”;
   it should be a tool that makes life easier.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.6.2. IMPACT ON THE AUTOMATION PROJECT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Positive factors:**
- The team is ready for change if it is justified and brings value.
- Written culture facilitates the implementation of documented processes
  and interfaces.
- Transparency allows open discussion of metrics and models, accelerating
  decision‑making.
- High agency means people will not wait for instructions but will start
  using new tools if they see value in them.

**Challenges and constraints:**
- Automation must not create an illusion of “top‑down control”; the system
  should be an assistant, not an overseer.
- Any constraints must be transparent and explained.
- The ability for manual intervention and overriding agent decisions must be
  provided (e.g., an “undo” button).
- Interfaces must be intuitive and not require lengthy training (remote‑first).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.6.3. DESIGN PRINCIPLES TAKING CULTURE INTO ACCOUNT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. **Assistant, not replacement** – agents perform routine tasks, but humans
   can always override decisions.
2. **Transparency of logic** – all formulas (Account Score, Health Score)
   must be documented and accessible for review.
3. **Asynchronicity** – interfaces must be self‑contained, not requiring
   synchronous meetings (dashboards, written reports).
4. **Flexibility** – ability to adjust thresholds, weights, and playbooks
   without involving developers (self‑service for RevOps).
5. **Training and documentation** – materials explaining how the system works
   must be prepared in simple language (simple language).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.6.4. EXAMPLES OF INCORPORATING CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS INTO THE ARCHITECTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

- **Control panel** – a dashboard showing agent load, allowing operators
  to see what is happening and intervene if necessary.
- **Documented triggers** – a list of all automatic actions
  (Account Score >0.7 → Strategist task) is available in the handbook.
- **Ability to cancel** – any automatic action can be canceled by a human
  within 5 minutes (grace period).
- **Asynchronous reports** – weekly email digests with key metrics
  and recommendations.
- **Simple language sections** – for non‑technical audiences, explaining
  what health score is, why forecasts are important, and how triggers help
  sellers.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.6.5. CULTURE‑RELATED RISKS AND MITIGATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

| Risk                                     | Mitigation                                      |
|------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| Resistance due to loss of control        | Transparency, ability to override decisions     |
| Misunderstanding of agent logic          | Training, documentation, simple language        |
| Information overload                     | Phased rollout, pilots                          |
| Rejection due to technical failures      | Reliability, automatic recovery                 |
| Conflict between agents and humans       | Clear separation of responsibilities            |

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**PROJECT CONCLUSIONS**
- Sourcegraph’s culture creates a favorable environment for automation,
  but requires special attention to transparency, flexibility, and simplicity.
- The project must be built on the principles of “assistant, not replacement,”
  “documentation,” and “asynchronicity.”
- The success of implementation depends not only on technical execution
  but also on communication, training, and team engagement.
