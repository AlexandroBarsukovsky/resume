2.1. ABOUT SOURCEGRAPH (PRODUCT, MARKET, CUSTOMERS) – EXTENDED PROTOCOL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Product and its Evolution**
Sourcegraph develops a code intelligence platform that enables developers and
engineering teams to:
- Find code at organizational scale (Code Search) – semantic search with regular
  expressions, navigation through dependency graphs, and understanding of
  programming languages.
- Analyze hidden dependencies, data flows, and assess the impact of changes
  (Deep Search) – advanced static analysis that identifies vulnerabilities,
  technical debt, and non‑obvious connections.
- Integrate with AI agents for automatic refactoring, documentation, and
  vulnerability detection (Model Context Protocol, MCP).

The product has existed since 2013 (first open‑source release) and has evolved
from a tool for individual developers to an enterprise‑grade platform.
Key milestones:
- 2013–2018: open‑source code, community growth.
- 2018–2021: transition to SaaS, product development under managed hosting.
- 2021–2023: raising investment (Series D of $125 million, valuation >$2.6 billion),
  team expansion, beginning of GTM transformation.
- 2024–2026: active shift from product‑led growth (PLG) to enterprise sales.

**Market and Competitive Landscape**
The code intelligence market is in a phase of active formation. Main competitors:
- GitHub Copilot Workspace (AI assistant integration, but limited depth of
  dependency analysis).
- GitLab Code Search (part of a DevSecOps platform, but less specialized).
- In‑house developments of large companies (e.g., Google’s Kythe, Facebook’s
  Nuclide) – not commercial products.
Sourcegraph maintains technological leadership due to:
- Support for monolithic repositories with billions of lines of code.
- Deep language‑level dependency analysis (Java, Go, Python, Rust, and others).
- Open API and ability to integrate with existing tools.

**Customer Base and Profile**
Sourcegraph is used by over 2 million developers (including open‑source and free
accounts), and paying enterprise customers include:
- Stripe, Uber, Palo Alto Networks, Atlassian, Figma, Dropbox, Shopify,
  Datadog, GitLab, Slack, Reddit, Yelp, Lyft, Netflix.
Customers have the following characteristics:
- Engineering team size: from 50 to 10,000+ developers.
- Architecture: often microservices or monoliths with numerous dependencies.
- Requirements: high security (SOC2, GDPR), audit, SLA, complex contracts.
- Key pain points: slow code search, risk of breakage when changes are made,
  non‑obvious vulnerabilities.

2.2. CURRENT GTM TRANSFORMATION (PLG → ENTERPRISE) – EXTENDED PROTOCOL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Reasons for the Transition**
1. **ARR growth requires scaling** – PLG works for small teams, but to reach ARR
   >$100M, large contracts with predictable cycles are needed.
2. **Enterprise customers demand a structured approach** – complex procurement,
   legal agreements, security, training, dedicated managers.
3. **Competition** – GitHub, GitLab, Atlassian already have enterprise sales;
   Sourcegraph must compete in this space.
4. **Retention and expansion** – without formalized customer success, it is
   difficult to manage net retention.

**What Is Changing in GTM Organization**
| Aspect               | PLG Model                            | Enterprise Model (Target)              |
|----------------------|--------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Customer acquisition | Virality, self‑service, marketing    | ABM, direct sales, partners            |
|                      | targeted at developers               |                                        |
| Qualification        | Automatic (subscription)             | Analytical (Sales Strategist)          |
| Sales                | Online, cards, free trial            | Contracts, demos, tenders, MEDDPICC    |
| Retention            | None (customers leave on their own)  | Customer Success, health score         |
| Expansion            | Viral (users bring others)           | Account Management, expansion signals  |
| Technology stack     | Developer tools                      | Salesforce, Gainsight, 6sense          |

**Current Structure of GTM Functions (based on handbook and interviews)**
- **Sales Strategist** (formerly SDR) – analytical role using intent data (6sense)
  and product analytics (Amplitude, Snowflake) to qualify and hand off to AEs.
  Currently manual: strategists export data to Excel, assign scores, schedule meetings.
- **Account Executive (AE)** – manage deals, use MEDDPICC. Forecasts are updated
  weekly, but accuracy is low due to lack of structured data.
- **Customer Success (CSM)** – onboarding, training, retention. No automated health
  score; risk signals are identified intuitively.
- **Account Manager (AM)** – work with top clients, but expansion signals are not
  automated.
- **RevOps** – manage CRM, reporting. Preparing QBR and board packs takes dozens
  of hours manually.

**Pain Points (Diagnostics)**
1. **Fragmented data** – product, intent, and CRM are not synchronized.
   Sales Strategists spend up to 40% of their time collecting data from multiple sources.
2. **Manual routing** – account prioritization is done manually, reaction time
   to intent signals is 2 days (according to 6sense data).
3. **No health score or expansion signals** – CSMs and AMs work intuitively,
   leading to unexpected churn and missed opportunities.
4. **Inaccurate forecasts** – a simple weighted pipeline is used without
   macroeconomics or seasonality. MAPE = 20–30%.
5. **Manual reporting** – QBR and board packs require 10–20 hours of analyst
   work per quarter.
6. **Cultural challenge** – high agency, remote‑first, direct communication
   require written, transparent processes, which are currently absent.

2.3. PROJECT GOALS AND CONSTRAINTS – EXTENDED PROTOCOL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Main Objectives**
1. **Automation of GTM operations** – build an event‑driven system where data
   is synchronized in real time and actions are triggered automatically.
2. **Improve forecasting accuracy** – develop a model that accounts for weighted
   pipeline, macroeconomic factors, and seasonality.
   Goal: MAPE <10%.
3. **Manage customer experience** – implement health score, automatically detect
   risk and expansion signals, trigger playbooks.
4. **Reduce reaction time** – cut time from intent signal to first contact from
   2 days to 2 hours.
5. **Scalability** – the system must support ARR growth to $100M+ and handle up to
   10⁵ events per second.
6. **Integration with existing stack** – use Salesforce, Snowflake, Hightouch,
   6sense, Gainsight, Tableau without replacement.

**Operational Goals (KPIs)**
| Metric                                     | Baseline Value | Target Value     |
|---------------------------------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Reaction time to intent signal              | 2 days         | 2 hours          |
| Win rate (enterprise)                       | ~20%           | >25%             |
| Deal cycle                                  | 120 days       | ≤96 days         |
| Net retention (NRR)                         | ~100%          | >120%            |
| Share of customers in green health score zone| not measured  | >80%             |
| Forecast accuracy (MAPE)                    | 20–30%         | <10%             |
| Pipeline coverage                           | <2×            | >3×              |
| Churn rate (enterprise)                     | 5–10%          | <5%              |
| CAC Payback                                 | >24 months     | <18 months       |

**Project Constraints**
1. **Budget** – CAPEX not to exceed $2.5M, OPEX not to exceed $2.0M/year.
2. **Timeline** – first phase (pilot) no more than 2 months, full implementation
   within 12 months.
3. **Preserve existing stack** – cannot replace Salesforce, Snowflake, Hightouch,
   6sense, Gainsight. Modifications via API and custom development are allowed.
4. **Cultural constraints** – all solutions must support high agency,
   remote‑first, written communication.
5. **Security** – compliance with SOC2 Type II, GDPR, encryption at rest and in transit.
6. **Compatibility** – the system must work with the current version of Salesforce
   (API version 58+), Snowflake, Hightouch.
