**2.2. CURRENT GTM TRANSFORMATION (PLG → ENTERPRISE)**

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.2.1. REASONS FOR THE SHIFT FROM PLG TO ENTERPRISE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Limitations of product‑led growth (PLG)**
- PLG is effective for attracting individual developers and small teams,
  but does not scale to large corporations.
- Self‑service (online payment, trial versions) does not account for complex
  procurement cycles, security requirements, legal approvals, or SLAs.
- PLG revenue is volatile; long‑term contracts are absent, making forecasting
  and investment planning difficult.
- Viral and self‑registration channels do not provide the necessary level
  of control over customer experience and retention.

**Strategic reasons**
1. **Achieving scale** – to grow ARR above $100M, large enterprise contracts
   with predictable cycles and a high share of recurring revenue are required.
2. **Competitive pressure** – GitHub, GitLab, Atlassian already have
   enterprise sales; Sourcegraph must compete in this arena to avoid losing
   market share.
3. **Retention and expansion** – enterprise customers require a structured
   approach to customer success; otherwise churn remains high and expansion
   opportunities are missed.
4. **Product complexity** – the Code Intelligence Platform requires skilled
   implementation, training, and integration; this cannot be achieved through
   self‑service alone.
5. **Investor expectations** – investors (a16z, Sequoia, Redpoint) expect
   growth in the enterprise segment as a key driver of the company’s value.

**Current organizational structure**
- **Sales Strategist** (formerly SDR) – a team of 8–12 people, analytical role.
  They use 6sense for intent signals, Snowflake for product data,
  and Excel for prioritization. They manually create account lists and hand them off to AEs.
  Problem: 40% of time is spent on data collection and processing.
- **Account Executive (AE)** – 15–20 people, manage enterprise and mid‑market deals.
  They use MEDDPICC, but forecasts are updated manually, accuracy is low.
- **Customer Success (CSM)** – 8–10 people, handle onboarding, training, retention.
  Health score is not automated; risk signals are identified intuitively or through support tickets.
- **Account Manager (AM)** – 4–5 people, work with top‑100 clients.
  Expansion opportunities are discovered by chance.
- **RevOps** – 4–6 people, manage Salesforce, reporting, prepare QBRs and board packs.
  Time spent is dozens of hours per month.

2.2.3. PROCESSES: FROM INTENT SIGNAL TO DEAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Current customer lifecycle**
1. **Intent signal** (6sense) → Sales Strategist receives a notification, exports
   data to Excel, checks ICP fit (Clearbit/ZoomInfo), evaluates product activity
   (query to Snowflake).
2. **Qualification** → Strategist contacts the prospect (email, LinkedIn),
   explores needs. If interest is confirmed, hands off to AE.
3. **Demo and negotiation** → AE conducts a demo, uses MEDDPICC for
   qualification, updates the Opportunity in Salesforce.
4. **Contract** → Lawyers prepare the agreement (manual work).
5. **Onboarding** → CSM gets involved after the deal is closed, conducts training,
   helps set up integrations.
6. **Retention and expansion** → CSM/AM communicate with the customer periodically,
   monitor product activity (visually, without automation).

**Time estimates**
| Stage                              | Average Duration | Notes                                      |
|------------------------------------|------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| Intent → first contact             | 2 days           | Due to manual export and prioritization    |
| Qualification → demo               | 7–10 days        | Depends on contact availability            |
| Demo → proposal                    | 14–21 days       | May be delayed due to approvals            |
| Negotiation → close                | 30–60 days       | Long enterprise cycles                     |
| Onboarding (time to first value)   | 30–45 days       | Due to manual processes                    |
| Total deal cycle                   | 90–120 days      | Higher than target (≤96 days)              |

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.2.5. IDENTIFIED PAIN POINTS (BASED ON DIAGNOSTICS)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. **Data fragmentation**  
   Intent signals, product events, and CRM are not synchronized in real time.
   Sales Strategists spend up to 40% of their time collecting and processing data.

2. **Manual routing and qualification**  
   Account prioritization is done manually (Excel), reaction time to intent signals
   is up to 2 days. This is critical because competitors react faster.

3. **No health score or expansion signals**  
   CSMs and AMs work intuitively, leading to unexpected churn and missed opportunities.
   No automatic detection of clients ready for expansion.

4. **Inaccurate forecasts**  
   A simple weighted pipeline is used without considering macroeconomic factors,
   seasonality, or win rates by segment. MAPE = 20–30%, making budget and headcount
   planning difficult.

5. **Manual reporting**  
   Preparing QBRs and board packs takes 10–20 hours of analyst work per quarter.
   This distracts RevOps from strategic tasks.

6. **Weak scalability**  
   Current processes cannot handle growth in the number of clients and deals.
   Increasing the number of AEs would require a proportional increase in manual labor.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.2.6. CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS AND THEIR IMPACT ON AUTOMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Sourcegraph culture (from the handbook)**
- **High agency** – each employee acts as an owner, does not wait for instructions,
  takes responsibility for solving problems.
- **Direct communication** – honesty and directness, even when difficult.
  No politicking; escalation through arguments.
- **Remote‑first** – fully remote since 2019. All processes are built on written
  communication, documentation, asynchronous discussions.
- **Transparency** – salaries, levels, OKRs, plans, even financial information
  are open to all employees (through the handbook).
- **Love for developers** – deep understanding of developer pain points, respect
  for their craft.

**Impact on the automation project**
- **Pros**: the team is ready for change if it is justified and brings value;
  the written culture facilitates the implementation of documented processes.
- **Challenges**: automation must not create a “police” feeling; it should provide
  tools for independent work. Any restrictions must be transparent.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.2.7. INTERMEDIATE RESULTS OF THE TRANSFORMATION (AT PROJECT START)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**What has already been done**
- A Sales Strategist team (analytical approach replacing SDR) has been formed.
- 6sense has been implemented for collecting intent data.
- Snowflake has been set up as a data warehouse for product events.
- Gainsight implementation has begun (health score is being configured).
- Initial ABM pilots have been conducted.

**What has not yet been achieved without automation**
- Automatic account routing.
- A unified, synchronized data model.
- Accurate revenue forecasts.
- An automated health score and playbooks.
- Process scalability as the customer base grows.

**Readiness for further changes**
- Leadership (CRO, CPO) supports the automation project.
- The RevOps team is ready to participate in development.
- The technology stack allows integration enhancements via APIs and event‑driven approaches.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**PROJECT CONCLUSIONS**
- Sourcegraph’s current GTM infrastructure is in a transitional state
  that requires systematic automation.
- Without automation, further growth of the enterprise segment will be limited
  by manual processes.
- The company’s culture (high agency, remote‑first) creates a favorable environment
  for implementing automated, documented processes.
- The key pain points (data fragmentation, manual routing, inaccurate forecasts)
  must be addressed first.
