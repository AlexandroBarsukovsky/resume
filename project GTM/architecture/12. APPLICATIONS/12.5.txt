**12.5. SIMPLE LANGUAGE FOR NON‑TECHNICAL AUDIENCES**

This section is created for those who want to quickly understand how the new
GTM system works without diving into technical details. Here we explain key
concepts in simple words, using everyday analogies.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
12.5.1. WHAT IS THE AGENT HOLDING?
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Imagine a **large factory** where thousands of robots work (we call them “agents”).
The robots do not replace people; they help them by doing boring, repetitive work.

- **On the first floor** there are scout robots (1,024 of them). They gather
  information from everywhere: who is searching for our products online, which companies
  are actively using Sourcegraph, which are the best fit for us. They assign each
  company a score and say: “Call this one urgently, this one can wait.”

- **On the second floor** there are three strategist robots. They decide which
  tactic to choose: conservative (retain and avoid risk), balanced (grow moderately),
  or aggressive (go all in, capture the market). They choose a strategy for each
  customer based on their behaviour and the company’s goals.

- **On the third floor** there are 96 specialised groups. Each knows its industry
  (finance, technology, retail), its region, and customer size. They tailor emails,
  prices, and approaches to a specific audience.

- **On the fourth floor** there are 3,000 executor robots. They do all the dirty work:
  * 500 robots process interest signals (who is searching for our product).
  * 800 manage the sales funnel (create tasks, remind about meetings).
  * 700 monitor customer health (if someone stops using the product – alert immediately).
  * 600 calculate money, commissions, forecasts.
  * 400 prepare reports for leadership.

And the human dispatchers sit at the control panel, watching screens and managing
this whole operation: setting goals, changing rules, handling complex cases.

**The main idea:** robots do the routine, people do what really matters – talking
to customers, strategy, and creativity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
12.5.2. HOW DOES HEALTH SCORE WORK?
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Health Score** is a “traffic light” for a customer. The system constantly monitors
how the customer uses the product, whether they contact support, whether they
participate in webinars. Based on this, each customer receives a score from 0 to 1.

- **Green light (0.8–1.0)** – the customer is happy, actively uses the product,
  risk of churn is low. We can offer them additional features (upsell).
- **Yellow light (0.5–0.8)** – there are minor issues. We need to pay attention,
  talk to the customer, find out what’s wrong.
- **Red light (below 0.5)** – the customer is about to leave. The system automatically
  creates a task for the Customer Success Manager (CSM), sends an email to the customer
  offering help. If nothing changes within a few days, the manager is escalated.

**Why is this needed?** Previously, managers learned about problems when the customer
was already leaving. Now the system alerts us early, and we can intervene before it’s
too late. It’s like an oil pressure sensor in a car: if the light comes on, you go
to a service station, you don’t wait for the engine to seize.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
12.5.3. WHY ARE FORECASTS IMPORTANT?
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Imagine you are planning your family budget for the year. You know how much money
will come in, when you need to pay rent, for school, for vacation. Without this,
you either overspend or run out of money at the worst time.

In business, it’s the same. To hire new salespeople on time, plan advertising
campaigns, meet investor commitments, you need to know in advance how much money
we will earn in the next quarter.

Our system forecasts revenue based on:
- Current negotiations (which deals we are working on, what stage they are at).
- History of successful deals (how often we close similar types of deals).
- The economic situation (if there is a crisis, companies spend less).

Forecast accuracy is above 90%. This means we make mistakes less than 10% of the time,
which is very good for business.

**The main point:** accurate forecasts allow us to plan the future confidently,
rather than guess.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
12.5.4. HOW DO TRIGGERS HELP SELLERS?
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**Triggers** are automatic rules “if A happens, then do B”.
They work like a smart assistant that tells you what needs to be done.

Examples:

- **“Interest” trigger**  
  *If* a potential customer starts searching online for “code search”
  or “code intelligence”, *then* the system immediately creates a task for the
  analyst (Sales Strategist): “Call Ivan Ivanov, he is looking for a solution.”

- **“Risk” trigger**  
  *If* a customer stops using the product for 14 days, *then* the system
  automatically includes them in a mailing list with useful materials and reminds
  the manager to reach out.

- **“Expansion” trigger**  
  *If* a customer is actively using the product and their number of employees
  has increased by 30%, *then* the system creates a task for the account manager:
  “Offer this customer additional licenses.”

- **“Pipeline alert” trigger**  
  *If* at the beginning of the quarter we see that there are too few deals in the
  pipeline, *then* the system sends a warning to leadership: “We urgently need to
  increase the marketing budget, otherwise the plan will not be met.”

**Why is this useful for sellers?** Instead of having to browse customer lists
every day hoping to find something, the seller receives ready‑made tasks only when
they are actually needed. This saves time and allows them to focus on negotiations
rather than searching for information.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**CONCLUSIONS FOR NON‑TECHNICAL AUDIENCES**

- The agent holding is a factory of helper robots that take over routine tasks.
- Health Score is a traffic light showing how a customer is feeling.
- Forecasts are a navigation tool that helps plan budgets and resources.
- Triggers are smart reminders that tell you what to do and when.

All these tools work together so we can react faster, plan more accurately,
and spend less time on boring work.
