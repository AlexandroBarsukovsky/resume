**1. SPACE OF REALITIES AS AN ALGEBRAIC STRUCTURE**

**1.1. Definition of a Metric Space**

**1.1.1. Hypercube and Its Dimensions**
   **1.1.1.1. Formal Definition**
   Let `(ℝ¹², d)` be a 12‑dimensional Euclidean space with an orthonormal basis `{e₁,…,e₁₂}`. Coordinates `x_i` belong to intervals: for the first five dimensions `[-1,1]`, for the remaining – `[0,1]`. Each dimension has a physical‑economic meaning:
      - `x₁` (Ethics) – degree of constraint by ethical norms (0 – utilitarianism, 1 – strict deontology).
      - `x₂` (Aggression) – intensity of competitive behaviour.
      - `x₃` (Prediction) – forecasting horizon.
      - `x₄` (Network) – degree of involvement in the partner ecosystem.
      - `x₅` (Creativity) – level of new idea generation.
      - `x₆` (Time) – time scale (0.1 – slowed, 10 – accelerated).
      - `x₇` (Probability) – degree of stochasticity (0 – determinism, 1 – complete randomness).
      - `x₈` (Constants) – stability of fundamental parameters.
      - `x₉` (Complexity) – degree of model detailing.
      - `x₁₀` (Scale) – size of the system.
      - `x₁₁` (Cognition) – level of abstract thinking (0 – algorithmic, 1 – intuitive).
      - `x₁₂` (Agency) – degree of agent autonomy (0 – strict control, 1 – full autonomy).

   **1.1.1.2. Metric and Its Properties**
   Metric `d(x,y) = √( Σ_{i=1}^{12} (x_i - y_i)² )` satisfies the axioms:
      1. Non‑negativity: `d(x,y) ≥ 0`, `d(x,y)=0 ⇔ x=y`.
      2. Symmetry: `d(x,y) = d(y,x)`.
      3. Triangle inequality: `d(x,z) ≤ d(x,y) + d(y,z)`.

**1.1.2. Set of Realities**
   **1.1.2.1. Vertices of the Hypercube**
   `V = {v₁,…,v₁₇} ⊂ ℝ¹²`. Each vertex corresponds to a specific mode of operation (e.g., `v_ETHICAL` – strictly ethical, `v_PROPHETIC` – prophetic, `v_NO_RULES` – unrestricted). Distances between vertices characterise the degree of incompatibility.

   **1.1.2.2. Topological Properties**
   The set `V` is finite, therefore compact. A discrete topology can be defined on it. Functions `f: V → ℝ` are continuous in this topology.

**1.1.3. Transition Matrices**
   **1.1.3.1. Construction of a Stochastic Matrix**
   For an ordered pair `(v_i, v_j)` define `P_{ij} ∈ ℝ^{12×12}` with elements:

(P_{ij}){kl} = exp(-|v_{i,k} - v_{j,l}|) / Z_{ij},
Z_{ij} = Σ_{p=1}^{12} Σ_{q=1}^{12} exp(-|v_{i,p} - v_{j,q}|).

Properties: `(P_{ij})_{kl} ≥ 0`, `Σ_{l=1}^{12} (P_{ij})_{kl} = 1` for all `k`.

   **1.1.3.2. Interpretation**
   Element `(P_{ij})_{kl}` is the probability of transition from the `k`-th dimension of reality `v_i` to the `l`-th dimension of reality `v_j`. If `|v_{i,k} - v_{j,l}|` is small, the transition is likely.

**1.1.4. Navigation in the Hypercube**
   **1.1.4.1. Problem Statement**
   For a given `v_s ∈ V` and a target point `x_target ∈ ℝ¹²`, find a sequence `(v_{i_0}=v_s, v_{i_1},…,v_{i_m})` minimising the functional:

Φ = Σ_{t=0}^{m-1} d(v_{i_t}, v_{i_{t+1}}).


   **1.1.4.2. Gradient Descent Algorithm on Vertices**
   At step `t` compute the gradient `∇_v d(v, x_target) = (v - x_target)/d(v, x_target)`. Choose the next vertex:

v_{t+1} = argmin_{v ∈ V \ {v_t}} ⟨∇_v d(v_t, x_target), v - v_t⟩.

This is equivalent to choosing the vertex closest to the direction of the gradient.

   **1.1.4.3. Convergence Proof**
   Lemma: If `v_t ≠ x_target`, then `d(v_{t+1}, x_target) < d(v_t, x_target)`. Proof: from the choice of `v_{t+1}` it follows that the angle between the gradient and `v_{t+1} - v_t` is less than 90°, hence the dot product is positive, and the projection reduces the distance. Since the set of vertices is finite, the sequence cannot cycle (strict decrease), so after at most `|V|` steps the nearest vertex is reached.

**1.2. QUANTUM SUPERPOSITION AND DECISION MAKING**

**1.2.1. Hilbert Space of States**

   **1.2.1.1. Basis States**
   Let `N = |V| × M × K`, where `M = 18` (number of modules), `K` – number of hyperparameter discretisations (e.g., 100). Basis states `|i⟩` correspond to the combination `(reality, module, parameter)`. Hilbert space `ℋ = ℂ^N` with orthonormal basis.

   **1.2.1.2. Quantum State**
   State of the system `|ψ⟩ = Σ_{i=1}^N α_i |i⟩`, `α_i ∈ ℂ`, `Σ |α_i|² = 1`. Probability of collapsing to `|i⟩` is `p_i = |α_i|²`.

**1.2.2. Problem Operator**

   **1.2.2.1. Construction of the Operator**
   Each branch (reality, module, parameter) has a quality score `Q(i)`. The operator `U_T` is constructed so that amplitudes become proportional to `Q(i)`. For example, using a phase oracle:

U_T |i⟩ = (1 - 2δ_{i∈S}) |i⟩, where S is the set of successful outcomes (conditionally).

In practice, `U_T` can be implemented as a unitary circuit that reflects a utility function.

   **1.2.2.2. Superposition as Parallel Search**
   Initial state `|ψ₀⟩ = (1/√N) Σ |i⟩` – uniform superposition. After applying `U_T`:

|ψ⟩ = U_T |ψ₀⟩ = Σ α_i |i⟩, α_i = Q(i)/√N (after normalisation).


**1.2.3. Amplitude Estimation**

   **1.2.3.1. Quantum Speedup**
   Let `a = Σ_{i∈S} |α_i|²` be the probability of a successful outcome. The amplitude estimation algorithm (an analogue of Grover’s algorithm) allows estimating `a` with precision `ε` in `O(1/ε)` iterations, whereas a classical method would require `O(1/ε²)`.

   **1.2.3.2. Application to GTM**
   This method can estimate the fraction of “successful” configurations (e.g., those where Account Score >0.7) without enumerating all `N` branches – critical for large `N`.

**1.2.4. Collapse and Extraction of the Optimal Solution**

   **1.2.4.1. Measurement Process**
   Upon executing the `[COLLAPSE]` command, the state `|ψ⟩` is measured. The result is some `|i⟩` with probability `p_i`. This is equivalent to randomly selecting a branch weighted by quality.

   **1.2.4.2. Deterministic Selection**
   To obtain the best branch, collapse can be repeated several times and the most frequent outcome chosen (the one with the largest amplitude). The error probability is exponentially small with `O(log N)` repetitions.

**1.3. PROBABILISTIC SCORING MODELS**

**1.3.1. Account Score as a Linear Combination of Factors**

   **1.3.1.1. Formalisation of Features**
   Let `I, U, F ∈ [0,1]` be normalised features:
      - `I` – intent signal strength (0–1), aggregated over 7 days with exponential decay.
      - `U` – product usage: `U = 0.6·(DAU/MAU) + 0.4·(active_features/total_features)`.
      - `F` – ICP fit: weighted sum of binary attributes (industry, technologies, size).

   **1.3.1.2. Linear Model**
   `S(w) = w_I I + w_U U + w_F F`, where `w_I + w_U + w_F = 1`, `w_i ≥ 0`.

   **1.3.1.3. Optimising Weights by Maximising F1 Score**
   Let a threshold `θ` be given. Then:

TP(θ) = |{i : S_i ≥ θ, y_i=1}|, FP(θ) = |{i : S_i ≥ θ, y_i=0}|, FN(θ) = |{i : S_i < θ, y_i=1}|.

F1‑score: `F1(θ) = 2·TP/(2·TP+FP+FN)`. Problem: `(w^*,θ^*) = argmax F1(w,θ)`.

   **1.3.1.4. Solution Using Gaussian Process**
   Model `F1(w) ~ GP(μ(w), k(w,w'))` with Matern 5/2 kernel. Optimisation by expected improvement (EI) yields the global optimum in a finite number of iterations (convergence theorem for GP optimisation).

**1.3.2. Health Score as a Composite Indicator**

   **1.3.2.1. Components**
   Let `U` (usage), `T` (normalised support tickets), `E` (engagement), `N` (NPS). Define:

H(v) = v_U U + v_T (1 - T) + v_E E + v_N N, Σ v = 1.

   **1.3.2.2. Optimising Weights by Minimising Log‑Loss**
   For historical data `(H_i, y_i)` (y_i=1 if the customer churned within 30 days), minimise:

L(v) = - Σ [y_i ln H_i + (1-y_i) ln(1-H_i)].

Since the function is convex (logistic regression), gradient descent converges to the global minimum.

   **1.3.2.3. Probability Calibration**
   Use Platt scaling: `P(churn) = 1/(1+exp(A·H+B))`, where A,B are fitted on a validation set.

**1.3.3. Statistical Properties**

   **1.3.3.1. Confidence Intervals**
   For estimates `Ŝ` and `Ĥ`, construct bootstrap intervals: generate `B` resamples, compute estimates, and take the 2.5% and 97.5% percentiles as the bounds of the 95% CI.

   **1.3.3.2. Asymptotic Normality**
   By the CLT, sample means of features are asymptotically normal; consequently, linear combinations `S` and `H` are also asymptotically normal. This allows approximate confidence intervals via normal approximation.

**1.4. FORECASTING MODELS AND OPTIMISATION**

**1.4.1. Logistic Regression for Win Probability**

   **1.4.1.1. Model Formulation**
   For each deal `i` with features `x_i = (stage, segment, region, ln(days+1), macroIndex)`:

p(x_i) = 1 / (1 + exp(-β₀ - β^T x_i)).

`β` is estimated by maximum likelihood.

   **1.4.1.2. Asymptotic Properties**
   As `n → ∞`, `√n (β̂ - β) → N(0, I(β)^{-1})`, where `I(β)` is the Fisher information matrix. This enables confidence intervals for forecasts and hypothesis testing.

   **1.4.1.3. Probability Calibration**
   Use Platt scaling: `p_calibrated = 1/(1+exp(A·p + B))`, where A,B are chosen to minimise log‑loss on a validation set.

**1.4.2. VAR Model for Macroeconomic Dynamics**

   **1.4.2.1. Vector Autoregression of Order p**
   Let `Y_t = (g_t, PMI_t, r_t, TechIndex_t)^T`. Model:

Y_t = c + Σ_{j=1}^p A_j Y_{t-j} + ε_t, ε_t ~ iid N(0, Σ).

   **1.4.2.2. Parameter Estimation**
   Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) gives consistent estimates. The order `p` is chosen by Akaike (AIC) or Schwarz (BIC) criteria.

   **1.4.2.3. Stationarity Condition**
   The roots of the characteristic polynomial `det(I - A_1 z - … - A_p z^p) = 0` must lie outside the unit circle. If violated, the model is non‑stationary and differencing is required.

   **1.4.2.4. Impulse Response Functions (IRF)**
   The impulse response function shows how a shock to one variable affects the others over time. It is used to forecast the impact of macroeconomics on revenue.

**1.4.3. Seasonal Coefficients and Final Forecast**

   **1.4.3.1. Calculation of Seasonal Coefficients**
   `S_q = (1/n_q) Σ_{y} ARR_{y,q} / ( (1/4) Σ_{y} Σ_{q'} ARR_{y,q'} )`.

   **1.4.3.2. Final Forecast**

F_final = ( Σ_{opp} Amount_opp · p(opp) ) · (1 + δ_macro) · S_q,

where `δ_macro = g_forecast - g_avg` is the deviation of the VAR forecast from the historical average.

   **1.4.3.3. Error Estimation (Delta Method)**
`Var(F_final) ≈ F_final²·[ Var(Σ w_i p_i)/ (Σ w_i p_i)² + Var(δ_macro)/(1+δ_macro)² + Var(S_q)/S_q² ]`, assuming independence of components.

**1.5. ALGORITHMIC BASIS AND SCALABILITY**

**1.5.1. Queueing System (M/M/c)**

   **1.5.1.1. Formulation**
   Event stream is Poisson with intensity λ (events/sec). Processing time per agent is exponential with parameter μ (mean 1/μ). Model `M/M/c`.

   **1.5.1.2. Erlang‑C Formula**
   Probability that all c agents are busy:

P_block = ( (cρ)^c / c! ) / ( Σ_{k=0}^{c-1} (cρ)^k / k! + (cρ)^c / (c! (1-ρ)) ), ρ = λ/(cμ).

   **1.5.1.3. Choosing the Number of Agents**
   Require `P_block < 0.01`. Solve numerically or use the approximation: `c ≈ λ/μ + z√(λ/μ)`, where `z` is a normal quantile.

**1.5.2. Caching Optimisation**

   **1.5.2.1. Response Time Model**
   Let `H(TTL) = P(record lifetime > TTL)`. Average response time:

T(TTL) = H(TTL)·T_cache + (1-H(TTL))·T_db.

   **1.5.2.2. Memory Constraint**
   Cache size `M` is limited: `M = N·TTL·R`, where `R` is the average record size. Problem: maximise `H(TTL)` subject to `TTL ≤ M/(N·R)`.

   **1.5.2.3. Solution**
   If the lifetime distribution is known (e.g., exponential), `H(TTL) = 1 - e^{-λ·TTL}`, then the optimal TTL is the maximum allowed; otherwise, it is determined empirically.

**PART II. ECONOMIC FOUNDATION**

**6. MICROECONOMICS OF DEMAND AND PRICING**

**6.1. Customer Utility Function and Demand**

**6.1.1. Random Utility Model**

   **6.1.1.1. General Formulation**
   A customer chooses between purchasing the product (`x=1`) and not purchasing (`x=0`). Utility:

U(1) = V - p + ε, U(0) = u₀ + ε₀,

where `V` is the value of the product (benefit), `p` the price, `ε, ε₀` are random components (independent, logistically distributed).

   **6.1.1.2. Logit Model**
   If `ε, ε₀` follow Gumbel distribution, the purchase probability is:

P(p) = 1 / (1 + exp(-(V - p - u₀)/σ)).

   **6.1.1.3. Interpretation in GTM**
   `V` corresponds to the expected benefit from Sourcegraph (accelerated development, reduced risks), `u₀` is the utility of the alternative (GitHub Copilot, GitLab). `σ` is the scale of randomness.

**6.1.2. Price Elasticity of Demand**

   **6.1.2.1. Definition**
   Price elasticity: `ε_p = (∂P/∂p)·(p/P)`. For the logit model:

ε_p = - (p/σ)·P(1-P).

   **6.1.2.2. Relation to Markup**
   Optimal markup (first‑order condition for profit maximisation):

(p - c)/p = -1/ε_p.

For the enterprise segment `|ε_p|` is small, hence the markup is high.

**6.2. MACROECONOMIC ENVIRONMENT AND CYCLES**

**6.2.1. Cyclical Demand Model**

   **6.2.1.1. Regression Specification**

ARR_t = α + β₁·GDP_t + β₂·PMI_t + β₃·r_t + β₄·TechIndex_t + ε_t.

Estimation uses quarterly data 2015–2025.

   **6.2.1.2. Economic Interpretation**
   - `GDP` – overall economic growth.
   - `PMI` – business activity in the IT sector.
   - `r` – cost of capital for customers.
   - `TechIndex` – sentiment in technology markets.

**6.2.2. Impulse Response Functions (IRF) from the VAR Model**

   **6.2.2.1. Construction of IRF**
   From the estimated VAR model `Y_t = c + Σ A_j Y_{t-j} + ε_t`, compute responses to a unit shock:

∂Y_{t+h} / ∂ε_t = (A₁)^{h} + … (in matrix form).

   **6.2.2.2. Interpretation for GTM**
   A 1% shock to the Fed rate (r) reduces ARR by 2–3% after 2–4 quarters. This justifies including `δ_macro` in the forecast.

**6.3. GAME THEORY OF AGENT INTERACTION**

**6.3.1. Signalling Game “Supplier‑Customer”**

   **6.3.1.1. Model**
   An AE (type H – high quality, L – low) chooses effort `e`, observed by the customer. The customer interprets `e` as a signal about the type and decides whether to buy.

   **6.3.1.2. Conditions for a Separating Equilibrium**

π_H(e_H) - c_H(e_H) ≥ π_H(e_L) - c_H(e_L),
π_L(e_L) - c_L(e_L) ≥ π_L(e_H) - c_L(e_H).

If these hold, there exists an equilibrium where different types choose different `e`.

**6.3.2. Cooperation in the Agent Holding**

   **6.3.2.1. Characteristic Function**
   For a coalition `S` of agents (e.g., analysts+strategists), define `v(S)` as the total benefit (additional revenue, reduced response time).

   **6.3.2.2. Properties**
   The function `v` is superadditive: `v(S∪T) ≥ v(S)+v(T)` for disjoint S,T (union brings more than the sum). Hence the core is non‑empty, and it is optimal to unite all agents.

**6.4. BEHAVIOURAL ECONOMICS OF INCENTIVES**

**6.4.1. Asymmetric Information and Principal‑Agent Problem**

   **6.4.1.1. Model**
   The principal (management) cannot observe the agent’s effort `e`. The agent’s utility is `U = E[w] - c(e) - ½ ρ Var(w)`, where `w` is compensation, `c(e)` is effort cost, `ρ` is risk aversion.

   **6.4.1.2. Optimal Linear Contract**
   Let `w = α + β·R`, `R = e + ε`, `ε ~ N(0,σ²)`. The solution:

β = 1 / (1 + ρ·c''(e)·σ²).

If `c(e) = θ e²/2`, then `β = 1 / (1 + ρ·θ·σ²)`.

**6.4.2. Behavioural Biases (Overconfidence)**

   **6.4.2.1. Model**
   The AE overestimates his chances: `P_self = P_actual + δ`. This leads to choosing effort above the optimum.

   **6.4.2.2. Correction**
   Introducing objective forecasts (financial agents) and linking bonuses to forecast accuracy reduces the overconfidence effect.

**6.5. INDUSTRIAL ORGANISATION**

**6.5.1. Product Differentiation Model (Hotelling)**

   **6.5.1.1. Linear City**
   Customers are uniformly distributed on `[0,1]`. Sourcegraph (`a=0.2`) and GitHub Copilot (`b=0.8`) have transport cost `t`. With linear prices, demand for Sourcegraph: `D = (p_G - p_S + t(b-a))/(2t)`.

   **6.5.1.2. Nash Equilibrium**
   Solving the first‑order conditions yields equilibrium prices:

p_S = c + t(b-a)(1 + (b-a)/3), p_G = c + t(b-a)(1 + (b-a)/3).

**6.5.2. Scale Effects and Entry Barriers**

   **6.5.2.1. Cost Function**
   `C(q) = F + c·q`, where `F` is fixed costs (R&D, platform development). Average cost `AC(q) = F/q + c` declines with `q`.

   **6.5.2.2. Natural Monopoly**
   If `F` is large enough, the market can support only one producer. This explains why Sourcegraph, having a large volume, can set prices above competitive levels.

**6.6. CONTRACT THEORY AND TEAM INCENTIVES**

**6.6.1. Optimal Contract for AE**

   **6.6.1.1. Linear Commission**
   `w = α + β·R`, where `R` is revenue from closed deals. The parameter `β` is as derived above.

   **6.6.1.2. Adjustment for Chargebacks**
   If a customer churns within a year, commission is reversed: `w = α + β·(R - γ·R_churn)`. This incentivises quality over quantity.

**6.6.2. Bonuses for CSM and AM**

   **6.6.2.1. Tied to NRR**
   `B = B₀·(NRR/NRR_target)` (capped at zero). If `NRR > NRR_target`, the bonus exceeds the base.

   **6.6.2.2. Accelerators**
   For exceeding the plan by 10% – multiplier 1.2, by 20% – 1.5, etc. This encourages extra effort.

**6.7. FINANCIAL MATHEMATICS AND RISK MANAGEMENT**

**6.7.1. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)**

   **6.7.1.1. Formula**
   `NPV = Σ_{t=0}^T FCF_t / (1+WACC)^t`. Sensitivity:

∂NPV/∂WACC = - Σ_{t=0}^T t·FCF_t / (1+WACC)^{t+1}.

**6.7.2. VaR and CVaR**

   **6.7.2.1. Definition**
   For loss distribution `L` (CAPEX+OPEX) with CDF `F_L`:

VaR_α = inf{ x | F_L(x) ≥ α }, CVaR_α = (1/(1-α)) ∫_{VaR_α}^∞ x f_L(x) dx.

   **6.7.2.2. Estimation**
   Use historical simulation (1,000 scenarios) or a parametric model (if normal). The contingency fund is set at CVaR_95.

**6.7.3. Real Option Valuation (ROV)**

   **6.7.3.1. Black‑Scholes Model**
   Implementing the GTM infrastructure is an option to expand. Option value:

C = S·N(d₁) - K·e^{-rT}·N(d₂),
d₁ = (ln(S/K) + (r + σ²/2)T) / (σ√T), d₂ = d₁ - σ√T.

Here `S` is the current project value, `K` the expansion cost, `σ` the volatility.
